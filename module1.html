  • Introduction to Oxford Nanopore Technology (ONT) 

    • 1.1 Principles of nanopore sequencing

         Nanopore sequencing is a cutting-edge DNA sequencing technology that allows the direct, real-time analysis of individual DNA molecules by passing them through tiny nanoscale pores. The technology is based on the principles of biophysics and has revolutionized genomics research and various applications. Here are the key principles of nanopore sequencing:

     

    Figure: Workflow on the Nanopore Platform Using a MinION Flow Cell

    (Image Source: Wang, Yunhao, et al. "Nanopore sequencing technology, bioinformatics, and applications." Nature Biotechnology 39.11 (2021))

    1. Nanopore Structure:

       Nanopore sequencing relies on specialized membrane-embedded protein channels, known as nanopores. These nanopores are typically made from synthetic materials or natural proteins, such as α-hemolysin or Mycobacterium smegmatis porin (MspA). The nanopore contains a narrow and cylindrical passage that allows single-stranded DNA/RNA molecules to pass through.

    2. Ion Current and Sensing:

       When a voltage is applied across the nanopore, ions from an electrolyte solution surrounding the pore flow through it. As DNA/RNA passes through the nanopore, it causes transient disruptions to the ion current. The movement of DNA/RNA through the nanopore causes changes in the electrical conductance, creating characteristic fluctuations in the ion current.

    3. Nucleic Acid Base Identification:

       Each of the five nucleic acid bases (adenine, cytosine, guanine, uracil, and thymine) has a distinct chemical structure. As the DNA/RNA strand moves through the nanopore, the different bases cause unique alterations in the ion current signal, creating a distinct electrical signature for each base. This enables real-time identification of the DNA/RNA bases during sequencing.

    4. Long Read Lengths:

       Nanopore sequencing allows for long read lengths, meaning that DNA fragments can be sequenced continuously as they pass through the nanopore. This feature is particularly advantageous for studying complex genomic regions, structural variants, and repetitive sequences that can be challenging to resolve using short-read sequencing technologies.

    5. Portability and Field Applications:

       Nanopore sequencing devices, like the Oxford Nanopore Technologies (ONT) MinION and MK1c, are compact, portable, and designed for field applications. This portability has opened opportunities for on-site and real-time sequencing in various environments, including remote locations and during outbreak investigations.

    6. Scalability:

       Nanopore sequencing is highly scalable, enabling different throughput levels to accommodate various research needs. Researchers can run a single nanopore flow cell for quick and small-scale experiments or scale up to multiple flow cells for larger projects.

    Overall, the principles of nanopore sequencing make it a versatile and powerful tool for various genomics applications, including pathogen detection, genome assembly, epigenetic studies, and personalized medicine. Its real-time nature, long read lengths, and single-molecule sequencing capabilities have revolutionized genomics research and expanded our understanding of complex biological systems.

    1.2 Advantages and limitations of ONT for pathogen genome sequencing

    Advantages of ONT for Pathogen Genome Sequencing:

    1. Real-Time Sequencing: ONT provides real-time sequencing, allowing researchers to monitor data as it's generated. This enables rapid pathogen detection in clinical or environmental samples, reducing turnaround times and aiding timely public health decisions during outbreaks.
    2. Portability and Field Applications: ONT devices like MinION and MK1c are compact and portable, enabling on-site sequencing in remote or resource-limited areas. This is crucial for real-time pathogen surveillance, outbreak investigations, and quick responses to infectious diseases.
    3. Long Read Lengths: ONT's long-read sequencing is ideal for pathogen genomes, especially those with repetitive elements or structural variations. It allows for better genome assembly and identification of large genomic rearrangements, enhancing pathogen characterization.

    Limitations of ONT for Pathogen Genome Sequencing:

    1. Error Rates: ONT has higher error rates, particularly in homopolymeric regions, leading to potential inaccuracies in base calling and assembly. Additional bioinformatics tools and validation may be needed for accurate pathogen genome reconstruction.
    2. Throughput: Although scalable, ONT’s throughput is lower than some high-throughput platforms, which can be a limitation for processing large sample sets. However, advancements are steadily improving this aspect.
    3. Basecalling and Data Analysis: ONT’s real-time basecalling and analysis are computationally demanding, requiring strong bioinformatics pipelines and careful quality control for accurate results.

    2.3. AMR Identification Using Nanopore Sequencing

    Antimicrobial resistance (AMR) has become a critical global health challenge, making infections harder to treat and leading to increased mortality, longer hospital stays, and higher healthcare costs. A variety of pathogens—including bacteria, fungi, and viruses—are responsible for causing infections, and many of them have developed resistance to commonly used antimicrobial agents. The inappropriate or excessive use of antibiotics has further accelerated the spread of resistance, making timely and accurate identification of both pathogens and their resistance mechanisms essential for effective treatment.

    Traditional culture-based methods have long been the standard for detecting pathogens in clinical samples. However, these methods are often slow, labor-intensive, and may fail to detect certain pathogens, especially those that are difficult to grow or present in low concentrations. Molecular techniques, such as PCR, have also been used for pathogen identification, but they are typically limited to detecting a predefined group of microbes, which can result in incomplete diagnoses and the misuse of broad-spectrum antibiotics.

    Recent advancements in sequencing technologies have revolutionized pathogen detection. Next-generation sequencing (NGS) offers comprehensive, culture-independent identification of microorganisms. However, it is costly, time-consuming, and prone to contamination from host DNA or environmental factors. Nanopore sequencing, a newer technology, has emerged as an effective alternative, addressing many of the limitations of traditional NGS and PCR. It allows real-time, long-read sequencing, providing faster and more accurate pathogen identification. In addition, Nanopore sequencing can detect AMR genes, enabling clinicians to predict resistance phenotypes and adjust treatments accordingly.

    Advantages of Nanopore Sequencing for AMR Detection

    • Rapid Results: Nanopore sequencing offers real-time detection of pathogens and resistance genes, significantly reducing the time required for diagnosis compared to culture-based methods.
    • Long-Read Sequencing: The ability to read long DNA or RNA fragments helps in identifying complete resistance genes and understanding their genetic context, such as their association with mobile genetic elements like plasmids.
    • Comprehensive Pathogen Detection: Unlike PCR or traditional methods, which are limited to a predefined set of targets, Nanopore sequencing can identify a wide range of pathogens and AMR genes without prior knowledge of the infectious agent.
    • Portable and Cost-Effective: Devices like the MinION are portable and more affordable than conventional sequencing platforms, making them suitable for use in various clinical and field settings, including low-resource environments.
    • Real-Time AMR Gene Identification: Nanopore sequencing not only identifies pathogens but also simultaneously detects AMR genes, guiding treatment decisions in a timely manner and reducing the risk of inappropriate antibiotic use.

    Nanopore sequencing offers significant advantages in AMR identification by providing rapid, comprehensive, and accurate detection of pathogens and resistance genes, helping to combat the growing threat of antimicrobial resistance and improving patient outcomes in a variety of infection contexts.

    3. Sample Preparation for Pathogen Genome Sequencing

    3.1 DNA/RNA Extraction from Pathogenic Samples

    DNA/RNA extraction is a crucial step in molecular biology and genomics research. The extraction method varies based on the pathogen type, sample source, and intended applications. Many commercial kits and automated systems offer optimized protocols for specific pathogens, ensuring consistent results. Other methods like Phenol-Chloroform Extraction, Silica-Based Column Purification, and Magnetic Bead-Based Extraction can also be used. Selection of the appropriate method should account for sample type, pathogen characteristics, downstream applications (e.g., PCR, sequencing), and available resources. Adhering to biosafety precautions is essential to avoid contamination and ensure lab safety during pathogenic sample handling.

    3.2 Quality control and quantification of DNA

    Prior to sequencing library preparation, it is essential to perform quality control (QC) and quantification of DNA/RNA to ensure the samples meet the necessary standards. Common methods include:

    1. Spectrophotometry:
       Using tools like Nanodrop, this method measures absorbance at specific wavelengths to determine concentration and purity. The A260/A280 ratio is used to assess purity, with ideal values around 1.8 for DNA and 2.0 for RNA.
    2. Fluorometric Quantification:
       Fluorometers like Qubit use fluorescent dyes that bind specifically to DNA/RNA for precise quantification. This method offers high sensitivity and accuracy.
    3. Agarose Gel Electrophoresis:
       This method assesses the size and integrity of DNA/RNA. Running samples on an agarose gel helps detect degradation, contamination, and impurities.

    When selecting QC methods, consider factors such as concentration, sample volume, and the sensitivity needed for your application. Proper QC ensures the success of downstream processes like library preparation and sequencing.

    3.3 Library Preparation Steps for ONT Sequencing

    Using SARS-CoV-2 genome sequencing as an example, two popular methods, Midnight and ARTIC Classic, are commonly employed. Both use a PCR tiling strategy to amplify the viral genome in overlapping regions, maximizing coverage across the genome.

    3.3.1 ARTIC Classic:

    This was the first SARS-CoV-2 nanopore sequencing method widely adopted by scientists. It amplifies the viral genome in ~400 bp fragments, which is ideal for RNA samples prone to degradation (e.g., samples subjected to multiple freeze-thaw cycles or stored at -80°C). This shorter amplicon length ensures better coverage, especially for degraded samples.

    3.3.2 Midnight:

    Midnight method is a faster and cost-effective approach. This technique is more robust against drop-outs caused by genomic mutations. It’s adaptable for both single-sample, on-demand sequencing, and high-throughput applications with minimal hands-on time, allowing automation.

    Both methods are effective, with ARTIC being advantageous for degraded samples and Midnight offering scalability and resilience to mutations. The detailed experimental protocols for both ARTIC Classic and Midnight are available online at ARTIC Classic Protocol and midnight-protocol respectively.

    Which Sequencing approach should I choose?

    The library preparation involves the following steps:

    1. cDNA preparation (~ 30 minutes): Preparation of cDNA from the RNA extracted from the clinical sample. Clean up the cDNA using 1.8x AMPure XP beads for ARTIC Classic protocol.
    2. Primer pool preparation (For the first time only, ~ 2h): Reconstitute the primers in TE buffer/Nuclease Free Water and prepare a stock of 100 micromolar and a working stock of 10 micromolar primer pool for each of the two primer pools. The consecutive primers have a short overlap region required to map the individual sequencing reads during analysis. Pool A contains the odd number of primers while pool B contains the even number of primers. This is important in order to prevent error in the Multiplex PCR step.
    3. Multiplex PCR (~ 4h): This step is for amplification of the genome out of the total cDNA which primarily contains cDNA of Human origin. Microbial genome is amplified in two sets of reaction using the two primer pools.
    4. Pooling the PCR product (~10 minutes): The two reaction sets are combined for individual samples. Clean up the cDNA using 1.8x AMPure XP beads for ARTIC Classic protocol. For ARTIC Classic protocol, quantify the purified PCR product using the Qubit dsDNA HS Assay Kit, and take equal amount (~200 nanogram) of samples for barcoding in order to reduce sample-wise variation in sequencing reads.
    5. Barcoding the sample (~30 minutes): Barcode provides unique identity to the samples which facilitates multiplexing, resulting in reduced processing time and cost. Depending on the protocol followed (ARTIC Classic/ Midnight), add the unique barcodes to individual samples. Keep a track of the barcode number and corresponding sample ID, which will be required for demultiplexing the sequencing data during analysis.
    6. Pooling samples and clean-up (~40 minutes): As the samples has been tagged with unique barcodes, they can be pooled in one tube now. Add 1x volume of AMPure XP beads to individual sample, mix and then pool the samples to avoid chances of barcode cross contamination. Quantify DNA concentration by using the Qubit dsDNA HS Assay Kit.
    7. Adapter Ligation (~10 minutes): Take forward the pooled barcoded sample for adapter ligation using the appropriate adapter. These adapters enable strand capture and loading of a processive enzyme at the 5′ end of one strand. The enzyme is necessary for maintaining millisecond-scale unidirectional single-nucleotide displacement along the strand. Additionally, by covalently joining one strand to the other, the hairpin adapter enables continuous sequencing of both strands of a duplex molecule.
    8. Clean-up of final library (optional): Clean-up the final library using 1x AMPure XP beads for ARTIC Classic protocol. Quantify DNA concentration by using the Qubit dsDNA HS Assay Kit.
